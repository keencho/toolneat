How to Test Your Monitor: Dead Pixels to Burn-in

January 28, 2026 · 8 min read

I bought a new monitor last year and used it happily for three weeks before noticing a single bright green dot near the center of the screen. Once I saw it, I could not un-see it. That experience taught me to always test a new display before the return window closes. Here is everything I wish I had known before unboxing that monitor.

Dead Pixels vs. Stuck Pixels

People use these terms interchangeably, but they are different problems with different solutions. A dead pixel is completely off. It appears as a tiny black dot on a bright background because the underlying transistor has failed permanently. There is no fix for a truly dead pixel short of replacing the panel.

A stuck pixel, on the other hand, is frozen in one color state. It might show up as a persistent red, green, or blue dot. Unlike dead pixels, stuck pixels are sometimes fixable because the transistor is not broken, just jammed. Running a pixel-cycling tool that rapidly flashes colors can sometimes unstick them. It does not always work, but it is worth trying before filing a return.

Running a Dead Pixel Test

The process is straightforward. Display a full-screen solid color and scan the entire screen with your eyes, section by section. Start with pure white, then switch to black, red, green, and blue. Each color background reveals different types of defective pixels.

White backgrounds expose dead (black) pixels. Black backgrounds reveal bright stuck pixels. The colored backgrounds help you identify sub-pixel defects that might not be visible against white or black alone. Take your time with each color. Rushing through it is how I missed that green dot for three weeks.

A web-based dead pixel test tool makes this easy. Full-screen mode, click to cycle colors, no software to install. Do this in a dimly lit room for the best visibility.

Attempting a Pixel Fix

If you find a stuck pixel, there are two approaches before contacting the manufacturer. The first is software-based: display rapidly flashing colors over the affected area for 20 to 30 minutes. Some tools let you target a specific screen region rather than flashing the entire display. The theory is that rapid cycling stimulates the transistor back to normal operation.

The second method involves gentle physical pressure. Place a soft cloth over the stuck pixel, then press very lightly with a blunt object like the eraser end of a pencil. Power off the monitor first, apply pressure for a few seconds, then release and power back on. This is risky because too much pressure can damage neighboring pixels, so proceed with caution.

Neither method is guaranteed, but success rates for stuck (not dead) pixels are reasonable. If the pixel is truly dead, only a panel replacement will fix it.

Screen Burn-in: Not Just for Old TVs

Burn-in happens when a static image remains on screen for so long that it leaves a faint ghost permanently. Most people associate this with old CRT monitors and plasma TVs, but OLED screens are susceptible too. The organic compounds that produce light in OLED panels degrade unevenly when one area displays the same content constantly, like a taskbar or a channel logo.

LCD screens are much less prone to permanent burn-in, but they can develop "image retention," which looks the same but usually fades after you display different content for a while. If you have an OLED monitor or TV, varying your content and using screen savers is not just nostalgic, it is practical.

To check for burn-in, display a uniform gray or neutral color across the full screen. Look for faint outlines of frequently displayed elements like the Start menu bar, browser tabs, or a dock. If you see shadows, that is burn-in.

Color Uniformity and Backlight Bleed

Not all monitor problems are about individual pixels. Backlight bleed is common in LCD panels, where light leaks around the edges, creating brighter patches that are especially visible in dark scenes. To test for it, display a completely black image in a dark room and look at the corners and edges. Some bleed is normal, but excessive amounts can be distracting during movies or dark-themed coding sessions.

Color uniformity is another concern, particularly for anyone doing photo or video editing. Display a solid mid-gray across the full screen and check whether the tone looks consistent from edge to edge. If one corner has a warm tint while the opposite corner looks cool, your panel has uneven color reproduction. Some monitors offer built-in uniformity correction, but not all of them.

When to Test and What to Expect

Test immediately when you get a new monitor, while the return window is still open. Most manufacturers have a threshold for how many dead pixels qualify for a warranty replacement, often three to five visible defects. Knowing your pixel status on day one gives you leverage if problems develop later.

For existing monitors, periodic testing every few months can help you catch developing issues early. OLED owners in particular should check for burn-in signs before they become severe. Early-stage image retention on OLED can sometimes be mitigated by running a panel-refresh cycle (most OLED displays have this feature built into their settings).

No monitor is perfect, and a single dead pixel on a 4K display with over eight million pixels might never bother you. But it is better to know what you are working with than to discover a defect after the return period has passed.
