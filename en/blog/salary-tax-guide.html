Understanding Your Net Salary: Taxes and Deductions Explained

February 5, 2026 · 8 min read

My first real paycheck was a disappointment. Not because the job was bad or the salary was low, but because the number that hit my bank account was significantly less than the figure I had agreed to during the interview. Nobody had explained the gap between gross and net salary, and I spent that evening googling abbreviations on my pay stub trying to figure out where the rest went.

Gross vs. Net: The Gap That Confuses Everyone

Your gross salary is the total amount your employer pays you before anything is deducted. It is the number in your offer letter and the number you tell people when they ask what you earn. Your net salary, sometimes called take-home pay, is what actually lands in your account after all deductions. Depending on where you live and your tax bracket, the difference can be 20% to 40% or more.

Understanding what sits in that gap is not just useful for financial planning. It also changes how you evaluate job offers, negotiate raises, and think about benefits. A $70,000 salary with excellent employer-covered benefits might put more money in your pocket than an $80,000 salary where you are paying for everything out of pocket.

Income Tax: The Biggest Bite

In most countries, income tax is the largest deduction from your paycheck. The way it works in progressive tax systems (used by the US, UK, South Korea, and many others) is often misunderstood. You do not pay one flat rate on your entire income. Instead, different portions of your income are taxed at different rates.

Say your country has three brackets: 10% on the first $20,000, 20% on income between $20,001 and $50,000, and 30% on anything above $50,000. If you earn $60,000, you pay $2,000 on the first bracket, $6,000 on the second, and $3,000 on the third, for a total of $11,000. Your effective tax rate is about 18.3%, not 30%. Many people mistakenly believe that earning one dollar more in a new bracket means their entire income gets taxed at the higher rate. That is not how it works.

Social Security and Pension Contributions

Most employed workers contribute a percentage of their salary toward a national pension or social security system. In the US, Social Security and Medicare together take about 7.65% of your gross pay (your employer matches that amount, though you never see it on your stub). In South Korea, the National Pension takes 4.5% from the employee side. European countries often have higher rates but provide more extensive social safety nets in return.

These are not optional. They come out before you ever touch your money, and they fund retirement benefits, disability insurance, and sometimes survivor benefits for your dependents. Whether the eventual payout will be worth what you contributed is a matter of ongoing debate in every country that has such a system.

Health Insurance Premiums

How health insurance affects your paycheck depends enormously on where you live. In countries with universal healthcare, a fixed percentage of your salary goes toward the national health insurance fund. In South Korea, that is about 3.5% of your gross salary from the employee, with the employer covering the other half. In the UK, it is folded into National Insurance contributions.

In the US, the situation is more complicated. If your employer offers health insurance, the premium cost is usually split between you and the company. The employee portion might range from $100 to $500 per month depending on the plan and whether you are covering just yourself or your family. This comes out pre-tax, which slightly reduces your taxable income, providing a small silver lining.

Other Deductions You Might See

Beyond the big three, your pay stub might include several smaller deductions. Employment insurance covers you in case of layoffs. Local taxes apply in some cities and states. Union dues are deducted if you belong to a labor organization. Retirement plan contributions, like a 401(k) in the US, are technically voluntary but often set up to deduct automatically.

Some of these deductions work in your favor at tax time. Contributions to retirement accounts, health savings accounts, and certain insurance premiums reduce your taxable income, which means you pay less income tax overall. Understanding which deductions are pre-tax versus post-tax can help you plan more effectively.

Why a Salary Calculator Helps

Doing all this math by hand for every job offer or raise scenario gets tedious fast. A salary calculator takes your gross amount, applies the relevant tax brackets, social insurance rates, and other standard deductions, and shows you the approximate net result. It will not be exact to the penny because individual circumstances vary, but it gets you close enough to make informed decisions.

I use one every time I am evaluating a new opportunity or budgeting for a major purchase. Knowing that a $5,000 raise translates to roughly $3,200 in additional take-home pay (after taxes and deductions) makes the math real in a way that gross figures alone cannot.

Making Sense of Your Pay Stub

Take a few minutes to actually read your next pay stub line by line. Most people glance at the net amount and move on, but the breakdown tells a story. Look at where the largest chunks go. Check whether your tax withholding seems reasonable for your bracket. Verify that your retirement contributions match what you intended. Small errors in payroll happen more often than you would think, and catching them early saves you headaches at tax time.

Your salary is not just a number. It is a pie chart of obligations, protections, and future benefits. Once you understand the slices, you can make smarter decisions about how to grow the whole pie.
